Maria Clara dos Santos

Maria Clara dos Santos, better known as Mão Santa (“Holy Hand”) or Mulher Maracujá (“Passion Fruit Woman”), turned 50 in August and has lived in Rocinha for almost 40 years. She came from Bahia to Rocinha with a young son.

While living in Rocinha, she witnessed a devastating rainstorm that caused landslides and nearly swept away her home. In the midst of that period, she says she encountered a man with leprosy and treated him using herbs. From there, her reputation began to spread by word of mouth through the community.

“I saw a man with leprosy and told him I had a remedy. He said, ‘Whatever you do for me is valuable because no doctor can cure me.’ I took him home and applied a remedy. The next day, the sores turned to ashes. And that’s how the fame surrounding the holy hand began.”

Maria Clara considers herself self-taught, but she also sought courses in herbal medicine to deepen her knowledge. Over the years, she says she treated people suffering from cancer, ulcers, psoriasis, bronchitis, and other diseases through home remedies.

In May 2014, she herself was diagnosed with cancer. According to Maria Clara, she turned to her own herbal treatments and later, when admitted for surgery, doctors told her they could no longer find the disease.

“If I cured so many people, why can’t I cure myself? I made my remedies. Later, the doctor said I no longer had the disease. The doctors were astonished and told me to continue my natural treatment.”

From Holy Hand to Passion Fruit Woman

After Rio de Janeiro City Hall demolished her residence in the upper part of Rocinha, Maria Clara could not forget the sound of the hammer blows destroying everything she owned. Her greatest worry was where she would live with her children. Out of anguish came creativity.

Remembering the stylized “fruit women,” she invented Mulher Maracujá — Passion Fruit Woman — a bold and humorous character with a yellow polka-dot outfit inspired by the inside of a passion fruit. What began as a way to bring joy and rebuild her life soon became a profession.

“I wanted to bring joy to the world. I wanted a truly cheerful character to cheer up the sick in hospitals, the elderly, in short, a project that would bring laughter to the world.”

She appeared on Brazilian television, including Qual o Seu Talento, and gained attention from international media as well. Her mission remained rooted in joy, healing, and human connection.

Maria Clara dreams of presenting Mulher Maracujá in her hometown of Santa Inês, Bahia. Today, she has turned her life around, built the Republica da Mulher Maracujá hostel, and welcomes foreigners from all over the world. On one of those visits, she was invited to participate in the film Favela Diaries.

Every shirt is more than a print — it is a connection to the street, the city, and the story behind it.
